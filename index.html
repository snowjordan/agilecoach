How often do projects fail? 

Failed IT projects

25% fail completely, 50% require rework, 25% don’t provide ROI, costing the US $50-$150B in lost revenue & productivity.

Poor ROI estimates

60% of companies don’t estimate the return on investment a project is projected to provide.

Unrealized M & A 

About 75% of expected growth via mergers and acquisitions never pays off.

Abandoned markets  

The majority of efforts to enter new markets are abandoned after a few years.

Pandemic effect 

58% of companies have had moderate or significant impact from COVID, causing project delays and cancellations.

Size matters 

25% of projects over $350K exceeded costs, while those with $1M budgets underperformed by 50%. Only 16% of these met requirements, with 31% being complete failures.

How often do projects fail? 

Ecosystem imbalance

Companies do not set their projects up for success or transform the business to support the endeavor.

Pressure to release

Internal and external factors contribute to pressure to meet deadlines even if a product is not ready for market.

Insufficient planning

About 75% of expected growth via mergers and acquisitions never pays off.

Volatility & uncertainty

Inadequate and unknown requirements, scope creep and frequent feature modifications from poor project management.

Optimism & bias  

Executives focus on company’s capabilities & experience, suppressing pessimistic views and not getting an unbiased external view.

Pricing should be aligned with competitors to avoid perception of low quality and risk of not gaining market share.

Too much complexity

Large systems with a lot of interdependencies often take more time and effort to modify and test.

Wrong audience

Shifting priorities

Team attrition

What helps projects to succeed? 

Reduce complexity

Break projects down into user stories, acceptance criteria, and a definition of done.

Good management 

Create a prioritized backlog, project documentation, and plans to communicate, mitigate risk, test, and measure success.

Manage stakeholders

Set stakeholder expectations, communicating delays and reasons as soon as possible.

Adequate estimates  

Set projects up for success by providing an adequate cost buffer and sufficient time to complete tasks and fulfill requirements.

Target the right users

Identify the audience that is interested in your product and preferably willing to pay for it, targeting with a convincing message.

Retain key assets

Keep the team and stakeholders engaged and invested, so as not to lose their experience and knowledge.

Manage risks

Teams should be pessimistic in identifying risks, their likelihood, and create mitigation plans to reduce risks.

Set milestones

Project managers should identify important milestones that will indicate the project is on track, and celebrate wins.

Get outside view

Bring in external consultants to provide an unbiased view, also called reference-class forecasting.

Decouple systems

Decoupling features into microservices, such as apps that run on a platform, but could be released separately.

Engage sponsor

Communicate early and often to ensure sponsor aligns with expectations and has real-time insight into status.

Avoid burnout

Team members should be dedicated to project, and should have steady workload to avoid disengagement and burnout.

Get more value with less risk more quickly


Instead of building for a long time to get to the `value, we build in short sprints, getting increasingly more value and increasing velocity as the project progresses. Best of all, by building more components every release, it’s actually less risky!  

Teams collaborate more effectively. More communication and information sharing helps ensure that we’re building the right solution. Because everyone is testing the product continually, both in staging and production environments, bugs are identified and prioritized more quickly. Team daily standups and weekly project leads meetings help remove blockers, mitigating the risk of missing sprint and release deadlines.

 Source: Agile vs Waterfall: Pros and Cons, Differences and Similarities (GanttPRO)

 

Greater visibility lets you know where you are

With waterfall, there are long periods where we can’t tell how much progress we’ve made and can’t get feedback. It’s easy to make big, costly mistakes that are hard to fix this late in the process. We also can’t pivot quickly if we find that we’re moving in the wrong direction.  

 Source: Agile isn’t Always Cheaper, But it Should Mitigate Risks

 With agile, we’re continually assessing requirements and have visibility at many points along the path. It’s easier to fix smaller mistakes earlier in the process. It also allows us to pivot quickly if we need to.

 Source: Agile isn’t Always Cheaper, But it Should Mitigate Risks (The Scholarly Kitchen)

Adapt quickly to change

Change can happen at any time during the course of a project, from a competitor releasing a new feature to disruptive advances in technology. 

 Source: ResearchGate

Although change is inevitable, it is important to avoid scope creep, when additional scope or requirements are added without changing the schedule, budget, or available human resources.

To combat this problem, teams implement a change control system, wherein all changes are rated based on the level of effort vs expected value they will bring.

Define requirements & acceptance criteria

Project planning entails:

  • defining the requirements
  • breaking work into user stories
  • creating a definition of done

 Source: Difference Between Acceptance Criteria and Definition of Done (JadeALM)

Acceptance criteria:

  • Requirements need to be clear, concise, verifiable, consistent, complete, & traceable.
  • Do not leave room for misinterpretation.
  • Weed out requirement conflicts
  • Ensure that it is clear how to track that each requirement has been satisfied.

 Source: Difference Between Acceptance Criteria and Definition of Done (JadeALM)

Break work down into user stories & acceptance criteria

We create user stories by putting the user in the story. User stories are units of work that make up the project. Sprints are usually 2-4 weeks. Ideally, a story can be completed in 1-2 sprints.

Example:
As a student, I want to be able to forecast my grade based on my current progress.

Before the sprint starts, the team should understand the acceptance criteria to satisfy the user story:

  • All grades for a class should be in one location
  • Users can enter potential grade values in all assignments
  • Potential grade values should be calculated to a letter grade, according to school’s grading system

Engage stakeholders

Project managers need to work with stakeholders to:

  • determine requirements,
  • manage expectations,
  • resolve issues,
  • negotiate conflicts,
  • prioritize functionalities and features,
  • and make decisions with stakeholder buy-in

 Source: iStock

You have to have soft skills: listening attentively, building relationships, being empathetic, and managing conflicts. They also need leadership skills to create the vision, prioritize and resource appropriately, and adapt to change quickly.

Create communications strategy

Better results & performance:

Companies see 3.5x better results with effective internal communication. Informed employees outperform peers by 77%.

What should be in the plan:

Detail the type of communication, who will receive it, how it will be provided, what will be in it, who will provide it, as well as any expected milestones.

Project sponsor:

Making the executive decisions and giving the project team an executive level point of view.

Team:

Responsible for the creation of the project and all phases of the project plan.

Other stakeholders:

Wide variety of people who are either impacted or invested into the creation of the project.

Communication with stakeholders

Understand stakeholders:

Understand their feelings, beliefs, & values, as well as their interest in & expectations of the project, and their position & influence.

Identify most important stakeholders:

Involve them in creating project vision, identifying benefits, goals, & metrics, planning the project and measuring success.

Mitigate stakeholder risk:

Engage stakeholders & manage their expectations of both positive and negative stakeholders. Identify success metrics like feature adoption or user sentiment.

Clear, trustworthy, & timely updates:

Ensure that stakeholders receive project updates, gauge their reactions, identify areas to improve.

Leverage soft skills:

Engage in active listening, relationship building, negotiation & conflict management to balance needs and make trade-offs.

Measure success via feedback and retention

  • Stakeholder engagement: Make sure the team is engaged and invested in the solution.
  • Feature adoption: Measure the success of new features and functionality via usage and usability testing
  • Task success: Perform A/B testing to test hypotheses between two solutions to see which performs better in time on task and task success.
  • User sentiment & satisfaction: After usability testing, provide surveys to understand overall user sentiment on the UX and task-level perceived ease-of-use.
  • Training & support: Provide seamless support, training, and docs to ensure your users are successful.
  • User retention: Benchmark user retention with current system vs new one.
  • Referrals: Happy customers invite others, increasing company growth.
  • Retain employees: Happy employees reduce risk of lost knowledge and experience

 Source: Touchwork

Scrum: Stay on track with daily meetings

  • Control over empirical processes:
    Focuses on transparency, adaptation, and frequent evaluation to continually test and improve at each stage of the process.
  • Self-organization:
    Each team member must adopt Scrum, so must have high level of independence and be able to self-organize.
  • Collaboration:
    Teams share responsibility and accountability.
  • Value-based prioritization:
    Adapt to and address new demands and requirements. Constantly evaluate and reprioritize each task.
  • Time-boxing:
    Sprint is two weeks long with daily standup meetings (usually 15-30 minutes).
  • Iterative development:
    Constant improvement and flexibility facilitate a high-quality product.

 Source: VectorStock

Kanban: Be transparent

    Principles
  • Start with current state
  • Pursue incremental, evolutionary change,
  • Respect process, roles, responsibilities & titles
  • Encourage acts of leadership at all levels

 Source: School of Managment

  • Visualize the work, the workflow, and the business risks:
    Identify areas that need improvement. Understand how to get an item to completion and optimize workflow.
  • Limit Work in Progress (WIP):
    Cut out wasted time and resources to maximize efficiency.
  • Manage flow:
    Focus on task flow to identify and remove bottlenecks.
  • Make policies explicit:
    Define and share processes and policies so everyone understands how work is done, what the goals are, and maintains objectivity in making decisions
  • Improve collaboratively, evolve experimentally:
    Teams must have a shared knowledge and understanding of problems. Use scientific model to experiment, analyze, & improve.
  • Iterative development:
    Constant improvement and flexibility facilitate a high-quality product.

eXtreme Programming: Tell the truth; no excuses

Values & Practices

Simplicity:

Do only what has been asked for. Every step is broken down into small, achievable goals

Pressure to release

Work together on everything, including requirements, coding, daily standups, and addressing issues immediately.

Consistent, constructive feedback:

Adapt process to project and customer needs, demoing software often to get feedback and make changes.

Respect:

“All for one and one for all”: Each team member is respected for their contributions, and respects customer opinions.

Courage:

Take responsibility for your work, and tell the truth about progress—no excuses. Value teamwork over ego.

Planning:

Create user stories with business value and priority of each feature. Only enough detail to determine time to implement.

Managing:

Project manager sets sustainable pace, daily standups, measures velocity, reassigns work, adjusts processes.

Designing:

Start with simplest design that takes less time. Refactor code often. Perform spikes to explore potential solutions.

Coding:

Everyone reviews code, adds functionality, fixes bugs, or refactores. Practice paired programming and commit code often.

Testing:

Performs unit tests and fix bugs before code is released. Run acceptance tests often,

 

Feature-driven development (FDD): Rely on docs

Unlike Scrum, FDD is focused on features instead of deliverables. Each feature is essentially a customer value-added user story. Because the team creates and relies on documentation to share information, they don’t meet as frequently and it reduces confusion and rework.

 Source: School of Managment

Stages

Stage 0: Gather data:

Learn the why, what, and for whom about the system to be created.

Develop overall model:

Draft outline using primary goal as guide. Develop domain models to merge into one. Add details as team learns.

Build features list:

List features that can be completed in 2 weeks’ time. These should be purposes or small goals, not tasks.

Plan by feature:

Plan tasks for each feature, assessing complexity to determine order of implementation and who is assigned. Feature teams have specific roles.

Design by feature:

Break down work into tech design and framework areas. Team reviews whole design before moving to next stage.

Build by feature:

Build user interfaces, as well as components detailed during tech design. Test and approve.

 

Dynamic Systems Development Method (DSDM)

DSDM sets cost, quality, and time constraints, then prioritizes features and scope to meet those constraints.

Principles

Focus on business need

Develop a realistic and solid business case. Prioritize requirements and features.

Deliver on time

Timebox to create trust between team and stakeholders.

Collaborate

Create psychologically safe environment. Get feedback from stakeholders.

Respect:

“All for one and one for all”: Each team member is respected for their contributions, and respects customer opinions.

Never compromise quality

Agree on quality standards and uphold them during the project.

Build incrementally from firm foundations

Define project scope to ensure common vision. Release features early to determine if heading in the right direction.

Develop iteratively

Features are tested and released throughout the project lifecycle, then improved by feedback.

Communicate continuously and clearly

Communicate with stakeholders to ensure the project meets expectations. Team collaborates via daily standups, simple documentation, & workshops.

Demonstrate control

Track and monitor the project’s progress using relevant Key Performance Indicators (KPIs).

 Source: ResearchGate

Scaled Agile Framework(SAFe)

Software companies generally have global teams, and many companies gain market share due to mergers and acquisitions, so SAFe relies on the proven practices and case studies of lean and agile, realizing that there is no one-size-fits-all solution.Per Gartner, SAFe is the #1 most adopted agile scaling framework.

Practices

Take an economic view:

Deliver best value in shortest sustainable time, balancing risks & costs. Provide guardrails for decentralized decision-making.

Apply systems thinking:

Optimize system components only when they contribute to the larger goal of the system and of the company.

Assume variability; preserve options:

Keep design options open longer in the cycle, using data to determine which optimizes trade-offs of revenue and costs.

Build incrementally with fast, integrated learning cycles:

Develop incrementally in short iterations, enabling team to get feedback, mitigate risk, and pivot when necessary.

Base milestones on working system evaluations:

Everyone shares responsibility to make the system successful. Evaluate at milestones throughout the lifecycle.

Visualize and limit work in progress:

Reduce work batches to increase throughput while still meeting capacity. Manage queues to decrease wait time.

Unlock intrinsic motivation of knowledge workers:

Ensure team members have autonomy to ideate and optimize. Deeper engagement creates better outcomes for everyone.

Decentralize decision making:

To deliver value quickly, empower employees by decentralizing decision-making to reduce delays, improve flow, get feedback faster, and create better products.

Organize around value:

To be competitive, a company must cooperate across functional areas to deliver value quickly and adapt to change.
